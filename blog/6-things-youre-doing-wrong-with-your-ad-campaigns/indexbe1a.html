6 things you’re doing wrong with your ad campaigns

  • Published by Elizabeth Yin -- Get updates of new posts here
  • 8 Comments

When I first started Shiny Orb, a site my co-founder and I created a few years ago, we decided to run a Google AdWords campaign to drive more traffic. I cringe when I think about that experience. We made all the typical noob mistakes and said, “Geez, advertising doesn’t work.” Four years later, I want to make sure that our customers don’t repeat the advertising mistakes we made. Here are 6 things you are probably doing wrong with your ad campaigns.

1) Your creative does not stand out.

If you are paying for advertising on a flat fee or a CPM basis, you need to make sure your creative stands out. So many people have banner blindness these days, so if you have a banner format to work with, you’ll want to come up with a creative way to capture attention.  Flip an image upside down. Upload a crazy image. Maybe make it look disgusting. Or beautiful. Use crazy language. Try something weird.

 image credit: br7tt

If you are using LaunchBitor Google AdWords (or some other performance network), this doesn’t matter, because you are paying only for clicks you receive.

2) Your creative is not specific.

Lots of advertisers will tell you that sponsored content, such as a sponsored blog post or a dedicated email, tends to be really effective. Why? It does well because you have a few paragraphs to explain the problem you solve and why people should trust you, etc. You have a lot of words to sell people on your product.

In contrast, you have very little space in banner ads or small text ads to present all the information you want. So a lot of marketers end up using that space to show pretty images or their logo. THIS IS THE WRONG WAY TO USE THAT SPACE. Unless you are Coca Cola, who the heck recognizes your logo??

 image credit: andy castro

You should be using the little space you have to make a compelling pitch for your call-to-action, spelling out how people can benefit from whatever you are trying to sell. This brings me to point number two.

3) You are trying to drive immediate sales.

This is what a lot of startups do. They’ll take out an ad campaign and will send people to their homepage and expect sales to come rushing in. This does not happen. People don’t know you. They don’t trust you. They’re not going to buy from you right away. Would you buy from any random person? You need to build a relationship.

The best marketers drive people to a dedicated landing page where the only call-to-action is to sign up for an ebook or a white paper or a lookbook or something.

One of many HubSpot ebook landing pages

Asking for email address is a simpler call-to-action that a lot more people are willing to do. Once you get that email address, you’ll need to build a relationship with your leads. The funny thing is that a lot of startups actually already know this as a best practice of advertising, but because they are so swamped, they don’t take the time to build a separate landing page or write an ebook or whatnot. So, they just send traffic to their homepage hoping for sales. This just isn’t going to work — you are basically throwing your money away if you do this.

Moreover, because ad spaces are often small, it’s a lot easier to write a small ad compelling people to sign up for your ebook. And, then later, you can use all the words you want to build the relationship and eventually sell to the subscriber.

4) Your call-to-action is confusing.

 What yvettesbridal.com used to look like

When you create your landing page, make sure to focus on clarity. You may think your messaging makes sense, but very often it doesn’t. Get a third party — a friend or relative or anyone really — to take a look at your copy and see if they understand what you mean. Make sure to have just one call-to-action and a limited number of links. The last thing you want to do is have a small, obscure sign up box, and a ton of links that can send your prospective lead away.

5) You don’t understand what kind of traffic you will get when you bid.

This is a big topic specific to cost-per-click (CPC) networks (such as LaunchBit ads, Google AdWords, and Facebook ads) that most marketers don’t understand.

First, you should bid whatever CPC makes sense for your business. This can be hard to know if you are just starting out. (more on this in the next point) That said, if the max you can bid is say $0.25, then you should understand what kind of traffic you are going to be getting. You will be largely getting traffic from developing nations. This is because ad networks price clicks differently — they realize a click from someone who is a professional who earns a high income and is based in the US is more valuable than someone who is unemployed, just started using the internet, and is in a developing country. If your product is strictly for people living in the US, you should make sure you use geotargeting filters. Even ad networks that don’t publicly display a geotargeting feature will often be able to do geotargeting if you ask them, but it will probably end up requiring a higher bid to get any meaningful traffic.

 image credit: MojoBaer

Users who are less sophisticated with the internet tend to click on ads more, and these people tend to come from developing countries where the internet is still fairly new. This is akin to how in the 90s, banner click-through-rates in the US were as high as 78%. So, even if your product can be bought anywhere in the world, you should realize that a lot of your clicks will be wasted. So, in some sense, yes, you want to bid what makes sense for your business, but if that number is too low, then ads will probably not be a growth strategy for your business.

Lastly, lots of marketers think that just because they bid high, they will get lots of clicks or their ad rank will be at the top This isn’t true. Ad networks factor in your click-through-rate when determining how many impressions to give you. Here’s a simple example of why this is:

  • Bob bids $2.00 and has a 5% click-through-rate
  • Tom bids $3.00 and has a 2% click-through-rate.

An ad network charges $2 * 5% = $10 for every 100 impressions with Bob but charges only $6 for every 100 impressions with Tom. So, of course, the ad network is going to favor Bob’s ad even though he is bidding 33% less!

Google AdWords interface 

CPC ad networks know that advertisers only have a small amount of wiggle room with their bids but have the ability to increase their click-through-rate by changing their creative. So, to incentivize advertisers to produce ads with high CTRs, most ad networks won’t charge you at your bid — they’ll charge you something lower. Typically, what you are actually charged is based on a modified 2nd price auction. So, if you have a high CTR, you will be rewarded by being charged a lower cost-per-click regardless of what you are bidding. This is the best way to get a lot of cheaper clicks.

6) You don’t understand your product’s lifetime value.

Lastly, if you want to use advertising to scale your business, you must have an estimate for the lifetime-value of each of your customers. This ca be difficult to compute as a startup, but simplistically, if you expect to make over $300 per new customer, then advertising is potentially a good way to scale if your customers can be found online. Here’s the rough math:

Spend $1000 on advertising:

  • $2.50 per click => 400 visitors to your landing page
  • 30% conversion on your ebook landing page => 120 signups
  • 30% open rate on your emails => 36 people read your emails
  • Eventually convert 20% of these readers to paying customers => 7 people become paying customers

=> $2100 in new revenue on a $1000 ad buy

This funnel is VERY DOABLE and repeatable if you have a Saas product. There is no reason why Saas marketers should not be just doing this kind of user acquisition, because you have the margins to play with. If you are charging something like $29.99 each month, and your users stick around for at least a year, you are making more than $360 in revenue on each user, making the funnel I described above easy even if your funnel numbers are not that good initially. This funnel can also work well for daily deal companies and products that charge a lot (such as insurance).

The flip side is that companies who have much lower lifetime values for their customers are not a good fit for growing their businesses through advertising. It’s important to understand or take a best guess about whether a) your customers are reachable on the web and b) if the lifetime-value of your customers is above or below the $300 proxy.
