Best practices for Keyword Search Research

  • Published by Elizabeth Yin -- Get updates of new posts here
  • 3 Comments

By Elizabeth

Keyword searches are a great way to learn about how your potential customers are thinking about solving their problems.  Many web marketers do keyword searches to scale their businesses via search engine optimization (SEO) and search engine marketing (SEM).  However, we’re going to talk about keyword searches for a different purpose — for market research. 

Before starting a web business, we want to know how to think about the landscape of a size given market — there are a number of techniques to do this research, and keyword research is just a small part of this research process.  However, it’s one of the more overlooked methods.  We’ll want to do keyword searches to learn about problems people are facing, current solutions, alternatives, and competitor presence.  We’ll look at this from two angles — searching for problems and searching for solutions.

Finding Pain Points

First, we’ll want to find out what pain points people are searching for online.  For example, suppose we are thinking about starting a web business around an online exercise motivation tool — we’ll call it Naggy.

To get a sense of the scope of the problem, we’ll want to know how many people per month are looking for words and phrases related to exercise motivation.  Google AdWords Keyword Tool is a great free tool that tells you how many times a particular phrase or related phrase is on average searched for in a given month via the Google Search Engine.  By experimenting with various phrases, using this tool, we find  there are 1000 monthly local searches for the phrase “How to get motivated to exercise.” By searching for phrases in just 3 min, we find there are plenty of other related “pain point phrases” that are searched for thousands of times per month, so this is a promising sign.  You’ll probably do lots of mad searching, but for LaunchBit Startup Guide users, make sure to save the “best phrases.”  At this stage, we usually sort our phrases by search volume and divide the list into thirds.  We’ll save the middle-third set of phrases.  These phrases tend to be both promising in terms of search volume but also generally have less competition than the top third, which will be relevant later for when we in take out ads (another topic for another blog post).  Also, the top third of keywords tend to be more generic, but we really want to use more specific keywords to get as targeted of an audience as possible. 

Now, let’s look at competition.  We want to know who else is offering a solution to this problem, and we also want to know what keywords those competitor sites are optimized for.  We can start by doing a Google search to find domains who rank high for the key phrases we saved from the Google AdWords Keyword Tool.  We can take some of these top domains and search for them in SEMRush, a freemium product, to figure out what keyword phrases those sites are optimized for.  Then, we can revise our Google AdWords Keyword search to see what other phrases may be relevant that we had originally missed. Note: if you are not paying for this tool, they limit you to 10 searches per day with a registered free account.

So now we’ve found the scope of the problem and some top competitors and their keywords.  Lastly, we’ll want to understand if there’s an underserved opportunity and get a sense of how many sites are trying to compete in this niche.  To better understand this, we pay for and use WordTracker Pro, but they also have a 7-day free trial.  Using WordTracker, we’ll search for the same saved keywords and phrases.  WordTracker gives us the number called “Google Count,” which tells us how many sites are trying to get your potential customers’ attention.  The Competition (IAAT) tells us how many sites have a particular keyword or phrase in their title tags and anchor text.  This indicates the number of sites that are going directly after that specific keyword phrase.  To make this easier to understand, WordTracker has created a metric called a KEI, which is essentially the ratio between the search volume and the IAAT number.  The math behind this is another topic in itself, but in essence, the KEI explains how underserved the opportunity is.  The larger the number, the greater the opportunity to “capture” this keyword phrase. 

Scoping out Competitive Solutions and Alternatives

Now we’d want to repeat this exercise with these same three keyword tools.  This time, we’d look for current solutions and alternatives.  For Naggy, we’d probably want to search for phrases like “kettlebell workout” or “online personal trainer”.

From personal experience, keyword research is great way to scope out the landscape of your potential market using a bottoms-up market sizing approach.
