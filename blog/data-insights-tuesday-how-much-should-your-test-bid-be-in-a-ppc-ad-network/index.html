Data Insights Tuesday: How much should your test bid be in a PPC ad network?

  • Published by Elizabeth Yin -- Get updates of new posts here
  • 2 Comments

By Elizabeth

Last week, I wrote about how much budget you should test with when trying out a new ad channel.  But, a number of new advertisers always ask me how much they should bid when testing a new channel.

image credit: bsabarnowl

A few thoughts on bidding:

Do the math

If you have a cost-per-lead (CPL) goal and have a general idea of your landing page’s conversion rate, then you’re in great shape. Let’s say that your CPL goal is $20. And, let’s say your landing page converts at 20% — meaning 20/100 people who come to your landing page sign up for your offer.

image credit: Wanda Dechant

Using these average numbers, this means that 1/5 (= 20%) people will convert on your landing page. This means that you need to send 5 people to your landing page on average before someone signs up. So, if a PPC ad network sends you 5 people who will lead to one conversion, $20/5 = $4 per click is roughly what you want to bid.

“But I want the cheapest leads possible!”

Ok, so we can all do the math. $4 per click is the max we can bid. Perhaps this number will change as we start to get specific information about this channel. But, the reason advertisers ask me about their bid prices isn’t that they can’t do the math. It’s that they want inside information. They want to get the cheapest leads possible, of course.

image credit: Joel Abroad

So, I’m going to give you a little inside information: PPC ad networks favor campaigns with high click-through-rates.

Let me explain.  We want to give you the best bid prices. And if your ads are appealing, then we reward you with much lower prices. Imagine we have two advertisers:

Ad A is bidding a maximum of $100 per click but has a 0% click-through-rate.
Ad B is bidding a maximum of $1 per click but 1% click-through-rate.

Who are we going to prefer? Given that PPC ad networks and their publishers only make money on clicks, they will favor the latter over and over. Now, obviously it’s an extreme case to get an advertiser who yields 0 clicks.

But, let’s say that Ad C is bidding a maximum of $0.50 per click and has a 5% click-through-rate.

Suppose there are 100 ad impressions available that I could give to Ad B or Ad C. Who do I choose?

Ad B $1 * 1 click = $1
Ad C $0.50 * 5 clicks = $2.50

Even though Ad C is bidding less, Ad C will make my publishers happier, so I will favor him or her every single time. Now, the really interesting thing is that because my best alternative to C would only pay a total of $1 at best for those 100 impressions, I am even willing to give Ad C a price as low as $0.20 per click just to keep him/her happy. ($0.20 * 5 clicks = $1, which is what my alternative, Ad B, would yield) Since, I would rather take Ad C over Ad B, I’m willing to give Ad C a discount — as much as $0.30 per click in this case.

So, in short:

1) Compute what your test bid should be based on your CPL goals and landing page conversion rates. You can always change this bid price when you get more data.

2) Don’t worry about trying to optimize that test CPC bid initially, because PPC ad networks will do that for you based on your click-through-rate.

3) Focus on changing your ad creative like crazy to maintain high click-through-rates.
