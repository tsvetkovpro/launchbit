How to set up URL parameters to improve your b2b marketing campaign

  • Published by Lee Constantine -- Get updates of new posts here
  • Leave your thoughts

By setting up URL tracking to analyze and compare your blog content or various ad creatives and the different channels you place them in, you can measure the actions and behavior of your traffic and optimize your b2b marketing campaigns.

So how do you set up URL parameters? Keep scrolling…

For simplicity, I’m going to focus on setting up URL parameters for your blog posts, but keep in mind that this exact process can be used for any link you want to track, including ad creatives.

Let’s say you have two different blog posts.

One has URL parameters. That means that when someone clicks on this link, that visit will be categorized by Google Analytics how you want this information organized — based on the referral data for this particular blog post.

The other has no URL parameters. That means that while Google Analytics will attempt to classify traffic that comes from this source, it will not categorize the data how you specify. In general, GA does a good job of determining the source of traffic automatically, but I should point out that if you do NOT establish parameters for a URL you place in an email, GA will not be able to track it as coming from email. It will be labeled as ‘direct’ or not categorized at all. So it’s better to set up these URL parameters so that GA can collect the data you want and give you the analytics you want.

Image credit: spinnaker.com

Tell Google how you want to collect referral data

URL parameters, or UTM (Urchin Tracking Module) tags allow you to add extra information to the link you create. This extra information appears in the Google Analytics reports under Traffic Sources. GA is so smart that it will recognize any parameter (UTM) you place after the question mark and automatically populate your account with exact words you tell it to.

There is a certain way to formulate these. For each new parameter, you need to add utm_medium=. This is how GA recognizes and organizes your traffic data. Let’s break it down.

 

utm: you’re telling GA that you’re adding a parameter to the URL

_medium=: this is a pre-established term in GA that tells it how to organize data

 

After the equal sign, type whatever word you want GA to call this parameter in your specific account for this specific blog. If you place this URL in an email, maybe you want to type in ‘email,’ or if you tweet this URL, maybe you want to type in ‘twitter,’ or ‘social.’ It’s completely up to you.

*Note that this is just one parameter. You will establish at least three per URL. Confused? Keep scrolling…

For instance, this is your original blog URL:

http://www.launchbit.com/blog/why-your-headline-doesnt-hold-my-attention/

 

To begin, simply place question mark at the end: ?

http://www.launchbit.com/blog/why-your-headline-doesnt-hold-my-attention/?

 

And then establish your parameters: utm_source=social

http://www.launchbit.com/blog/why-your-headline-doesnt-hold-my-attention/?utm_source=social

 

Remember to put a symbol in between new parameters: &

http://www.launchbit.com/blog/why-your-headline-doesnt-hold-my-attention/?utm_source=social&utm_medium=twitter

 

By the time you’re done, the URL is very long:

http://www.launchbit.com/blog/why-your-headline-doesnt-hold-my-attention/?utm_source=social&utm_medium=twitter&utm_content=blog&utm_campaign=why%20 your%20headline%20doesn%E2%80%99t%20hold%20my%20attention

 

If you’re asking yourself where the numbers and percentages came from, don’t worry.  I’ll explain in a second.

Ok, so you understand URL parameters (UTMs) and how they actually work to grab information in your various marketing channels. This is good. Now I’m going to tell you that there are great tools out there to make this easier.

This best tool you can use for this is Google’s very own URL Builder.

You can make the Source and Medium what you want, but to stay sane I chose a description for my blogs and then stuck with it. If I want to put my link in Twitter, I use ‘social’ for Source and ‘twitter’ for Medium. I put ‘blog’ in the Content field since I haven’t specified this in any other field yet. The Term field is for you to specify your paid keywords, but not necessary since I’m not paying to promote this blog.

*Note that I always use lowercase so GA doesn’t group things in ways I don’t want.

The Campaign Name is what I use most often, so I just copy and paste the title of my blog into this field in the URL builder. This is the first thing GA displays to me when I click Campaigns.

*Note that the URL Builder adds %20 for spaces and GA adds dashes. So if I copy and paste the blog title, Why your headline doesn’t hold my attention into the Campaign Name, the new URL (with parameters) will look like this:

 

http://www.launchbit.com/blog/why-your-headline-doesnt-hold-my-attention/?utm_source=social&utm_medium=twitter&utm_content=blog&utm_campaign=why %20your%20headline%20doesn%E2%80%99t%20hold%20my%20attention

But you can go deeper

It’s smart to use as many fields as you can in the URL builder. It helps you go deeper and get a better idea of where exactly your traffic is coming from and which content they like. The actual URL is not going to display in GA (not the original and not the tagged URL), so you need to add the right information in the right field so that it displays correctly in GA.

This can get complicated. You’ll want to use the same field descriptions for the same types of content so that GA can lump them together. You don’t want something like this to show up Google Analytics.

These are all going to the same blog actually. This was back in my amateur days. Mistake 1 was that I made the Campaign Name two different things (e.g. blog ad blog7). Mistake 2 was that I didn’t consider the fact that GA default settings take into consideration capital letters (e.g. Blog and blog). So even though they are the same thing, one has a capital so GA thinks they are different. I’m all for learning by doing, but it can cause some headaches.

Remember to set up parameters for each channel

You’ll need a different URL with different parameters for each channel (as pictured above), but all these parameters link back to the same blog post so you know where traffic came from for each blog post.

Do this correctly, and you’ll be in a better position to determine where your traffic came from so you can place your blogs, ads, or any content you have in the proper channels and optimize your b2b campaign.
