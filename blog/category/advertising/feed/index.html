Facebook ads have gone through massive transitions over the years. In the beginning, the targeting capabilities were very simple: select demographic options and get your campaign in front of people who met those demographics. The problem with this was that you didn’t have the ability to go granular in your campaign. If you wanted to reach small business owners, they put your ad in front of EVERYONE who was even remotely involved with a small business. But what if you just wanted to reach say small business owners who were Constant Contact users? There wasn’t a way to get that granular.

Today, Facebook has an immense number of ad targeting options that most people don’t know about but are really powerful. Here are 3 ways to hyper-targeted business audiences on Facebook.

1) Retarget visitors to your site

This is the most well-known of the three options. You can cookie visitors to your site and then display ads to those people when they browse Facebook. You can set this up with companies like Perfect Audience, AdRoll, and ReTargeter.

2) Create custom audiences

Retargeting is a good way to reach high intent potential customers. But, the limitation to retargeting ads is that your max reach is the total number of people coming to your site. So, how do you get more reach but still hyper-target.

Custom audiences on Facebook is an area that I’m super excited about. You can simply upload a list of email addresses of people you want to target, and your ad will get in front of a subset of those people. Pretty amazing. Here’s how to do this:

A) In the Ads Manager within your account, click on “Audiences”.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

B) Click on “Create Audiences”.  Then select “Data File Custom Audience”.  Upload your CSV file with your list of email addresses — people you would like to get your ad campaign in front of.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

So what list of email addresses should you upload? You could upload your list of prospectives — people who signed up for your free trial or perhaps your ebook or white paper. Alternatively, if you don’t have a large list of prospective customers, we at LaunchBit can help you generate a list through our custom audiences program and run those ads for you.  This program is in beta.

3) Create look-alike custom audiences

Lastly, once you have a list of custom audiences, Facebook can help you augment your reach further by getting your campaign in front of other people who are similar to those on your list. Unlike their old targeting options, this feature allows you to decide how closely similar you want this augmented audience to be. Here’s how to set this up:

A) Once you have followed the steps to create a custom audience above, go to the Power Editor and select “Ad Tools”.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

B) Select your custom audience list and then press “Create Lookalike Audience”.

So you’ve decided to take the leap and think split testing is worth your time. Ok, so where do you start?

We all know what to split test

Of course, you want to split test whatever will guide your traffic to your call-to-action. Most marketers like to make adjustments to the most important elements – like form length, headline, and call-to-action – because they have the most impact on whether your traffic will convert or not.

I’m here to tell you that headlines are THE MOST important element to split test, and thus, that is where you should start. The headline is the most important thing that you can split test because it is the first “gateway” that your readers have to pass through. On average, 8 out of 10 people will read headline copy, but only 2 out of 10 will read the rest. This means that you will lose more people at the headline than anywhere else on the page, so test the headline first.

Since it’s the first thing that your visitors will read, the headline of your landing page offers the biggest opportunity for improvements in conversion rate. Make it compelling, because you only have 0-8 seconds to make an impression on your visitors.

You still think headlines aren’t THAT important?

Have you heard the story about advertising legend David Ogilvy, who rewrote this famous headline for an automobile advertisement 104 times:

This is someone who clearly understood the power of headlines, and how the headline literally determined whether his content would get read.

Here another example. Adam Mordecai of Upworthy (formerly The Onion) says that he writes 25 headlines for every post:

Of course, writing a great headline doesn’t guarantee success, but the better your headline, the better your chances of getting your content read and your visitors taking action. The benefit conveyed in the headline still needs to be properly satisfied with your offer on the rest of your landing page.

Every marketer would love to see an increase in clicks or conversions and we know that headlines have a BIG impact in making this happen.  Still, many many many b2b marketers underestimate just how important headlines are and how they can make a difference in getting more eyes on your content.

Headlines are a big deal.

Why, again? Because it’s the first thing people will see. Not the text copy, not the form, not even the call to action.

So what’s the takeaway?

Headlines are the MOST important landing page element to split test because they are what compel visitors to click, or read, or share, or convert. So spend considerable time writing your headlines and split testing to improve them. Many marketers spend A LOT of time testing various elements of their landing pages (button colors are overrated), but then just put any old headline at the top without realizing that in doing so they might not be ensuring that they are actually persuading their visitors to convert.

]]> So you’ve created a beautiful landing page, your call to action fits your purpose, and you’ve published it to the masses. You’re feeling relieved as you wait for the results to come back in hopes that you’ll be able to pat yourself on the back later. And then, your relief quickly turns to disappointment when you notice that you’re not converting as much traffic as you’d anticipated and you’re left staring at your mediocre results wondering what went wrong.

Image credit: b2bsecrets.com

This scenario happens quite often and leaves a lot of b2b marketers with a decision to make. To split test or not to split test. We all know what split testing is and that it can surely improve landing page results if it’s done correctly, but how many of us have actually tried it and received results we could take action with? I’m guessing not many. Even if you’ve been thinking about split testing for a while, you might be wondering just exactly how much time you should spend on it?

The answer really depends on the size of your company – how many employees you have to put time into it, how much you have to pay for it, and how much traffic you receive to your landing page on a monthly basis. The more of these things you have, the more practical split testing will be. The less of these things, the less time and energy you should put into testing. Let’s expand on the most important of these things – traffic. You first need to make sure you have enough visitors going to your landing pages that will give you significant results during your split tests.

So how many visitors are enough?

To get good numbers in your split testing efforts that will actually have an impact on your conversion rates, you should have a minimum of 100 visitors to each landing page and at least 20 conversions. This means you need enough visitors to increase your conversion rate. This also means you need enough conversions to generate good results. Without this kind of volume, your split testing will not be worth it. That’s the simple truth.  As you grow your traffic and increase your leads you can decide when the return is worth investing more of your time split testing.

A lot of B2B advertisers ask us what a good click-through-rate is for their ads. In fact, about 30% of B2B marketers judge the performance of their ad campaigns by their click-through-rate.  However, measuring whether the click-through-rate of your ad campaign is “good” or “bad” is complicated and sometimes impossible.

What causes high click-through-rates?

Let’s dissect what makes for a good click-through-rate: ad positioning, targeting & context, creative & copy, and ad format.

1) Ad positioning

This is the placement of the ad. On a website, banner ads are typically placed on the right side of the page. Sometimes there are banners across the top and footers of a webpage as well. If you hold other factors constant, the position alone will heavily influence whether people click on your ad. Afterall, the more prominent the ad positioning, the more likely someone will click on it.

With display campaigns, when there is a discrepancy in click-through-rate between two blog or newsletter publishers, it’s very often the ad positioning that makes up the discrepancy. In these cases, it’s hard to say that one publisher is “better” than another by looking at the click-through-rate alone. Sometimes discrepancies in click-through-rate based on ad placement alone can be orders of magnitude different between publishers.

2) Targeting & context

This is how well an ad network matches an ad with the right audience. Ad networks like Google use their search engine to match ads with consumers’ searches. Display ad networks tend to use context of the content to match ads with visitors.

3) Creative & copy

This is the ad creative that an advertiser submits. This matters a lot. Having a good product that excites people isn’t enough. Creating ads that catch attention, and constantly testing and refreshing ad creatives so that they continue to catch attention is important in keeping click-through-rates high.

4) Ad format

Lastly, ad format affects click-through-rates. Click-through-rates on banner ads have dropped from typical click-through-rates of 70%+ to < 0.1% over the last two decades.  In contrast, the click-through-rates on Twitter ads or Facebook ads are quite high, because they are prominently placed in their respective feeds and are made to look like the surrounding content.

How to benchmark click-through-rates

With all of these factors affecting click-through-rates, it’s hard to say that one publisher has a “low” click-through-rate simply because the relevancy was poor. It could be that the ad position was hidden. Or the ad format was a small banner.

If click-through-rates are important to you as a B2B advertiser, the wrong benchmark is to compare the click-through-rate across publishers. The correct benchmark is to compare the click-through-rate of your ad on a particular publisher with other ads that have been in that same adzone. This holds the ad-positioning and format constant so that these are not differing factors when you do your click-through-rate comparisons.

]]> By Elizabeth

image credit: Joe Lanman

I was chatting with my co-worker about what days of the week B2B marketers prefer to take out ad campaigns. He argued that most B2B marketers prefer to take out ads Monday – Wednesday.  Afterall, enterprise software previously required high touch sales, and sales teams and decision makers don’t like to work the weekends. I argued that with the rise in Saas dominating the B2B market and growing at nearly 20% YOY, B2B companies are able to acquire customers with less high-touch sales. So, software users can start free trials right away without a credit card — even on the weekend.

So, we turned to the data in the LaunchBit network to see who was right. Can you guess the most popular day for B2B marketers to take out ads?

It turns out that using data from our network (June – November 2013), overwhelmingly, marketers took out campaigns on Wednesdays. The caveat is that we measured these campaign start times based on Pacific time, even though we have advertisers from around the globe. So, campaigns that started on Sunday Pacific time, for example, may actually be started on Monday local time in say Australia, though this is not considered in this dataset.

Interestingly, not only did marketers prefer Wednesday by a huge margin, but the data also looks somewhat normalized. In other words, Tuesday and Thursday are the next most popular days to start campaigns, and the number of started campaigns decays the further you get from Wednesday.

Now, if you’re a B2B marketer, this data can help you, because if your product does not require a sales person to call down your leads immediately, then you are better off advertising at non peak times to get better prices. Hint: it is true that clicks are cheaper on the weekends, so if you want to take advantage of cheaper clicks, you should definitely think about starting a campaign on a Friday or Saturday.

]]> I discussed on Tuesday the importance of changing your ad creatives in order to get the best PPC prices.  But, how many creatives is enough to test?

This is actually a huge hangup for most advertisers I meet. Most marketers are too busy managing tons of different activities — no one has time to come up with a lot of creatives only to test a channel briefly and then scrap it all together. Here are 2 rules for testing a range of creatives.

Image credit: Digitalmarketer.com

1) Use a handful of offers

It’s worth your while to come up with a handful of offers that you can use to test every new paid marketing channel. For example, perhaps it’s 5 different ebooks. This is a technique that companies like Hubspot use.  They’ll generate a variety of different ebooks and whitepapers and test which ones convert the best. Trying just one offer does not conclusively tell you about a channel, because if people don’t convert to leads, you don’t have a sense of whether the problem lies in your offer or with the fit of the channel.

If creating content takes too long, you can try offering hot products or services as incentives. New Relic does this with their t-shirts and helicopters. AppSumo does this with their software contests. Whatever your offer, make sure you have a handful to test.

2) Your ad design doesn’t matter

One big reason coming up with multiple creatives is daunting is that most marketers are perfectionists about their creatives and will need time with a designer, and it can take months to get around to testing a channel. Don’t go down this path. It turns out that unless your target market is designers, you shouldn’t sweat creatives. In fact, we’ve found that in our own network, design matters less than specificity of an offer. For example, you are much better off writing in text (even if on a banner ad itself) what your offer is as opposed to showing an aesthetically pleasing graphical design.

Your audience wants to know what you are advertising — make it easy for them to figure out, and be specific. And, this does not require a designer.

If you follow these two rules, developing creatives to test new paid marketing channels should not be a bear.
