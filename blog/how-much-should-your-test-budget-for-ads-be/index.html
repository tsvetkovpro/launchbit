Data Insights Tuesday: How much should your test budget for ads be?

  • Published by Elizabeth Yin -- Get updates of new posts here
  • 1 Comment

By Elizabeth

“How much should my test budget for ads be?”

I get this question all the time from marketers. Turns out there’s actually a math-based answer to this question. Let’s break that down.

So how much should a test budget be?

img credit: 401(K) 2013

You should understand how many leads you want from your test to know whether to double down on a given ad channel. Unfortunately, this will vary by advertiser. If you’re a Saas company with product pricing at $50 per month, then you may have pretty high conversions to sales off your leads. In contrast, if you are selling a $50k product that has a much longer sales cycle with a small conversion rate, you may need more leads — and more of a budget and time — to see if your test is successful.

Let’s run the math

Say you run Widgets-as-a-service.com. Your typical package is $50 per month. You typically have a 10% conversion from leads to sales within 1 month. This means that at the very least, to prove that you get at least one sale from a given ad channel, you will need least 10 leads and a 1 month time period to know whether an ad channel is converting.

Let’s say that your average customer sticks around for 12 months. So your average lifetime-value for a given customer is $50 * 12 = $600. A good rule of thumb is to spend 1/3 to 2/3 of your average lifetime value in acquiring new customers.

img credit: Tobyotter

This means that you can spend between $200-$400. If you are cash-strapped, you should be closer to the 1/3 mark. If you have raised a lot of money, you should be closer to the 2/3 mark if you are trying to accelerate your customer acquisition process. For these calculations, let’s spend 1/2 of our LTV, which is $300, to acquire a new customer. This is smack in the middle of this range.

At a 10% average conversion from lead to sale, you should pay no more than 10% lead to sales conversion rate * $300 lifetime value = $30 per lead.

You with me so far?

Now, since you need at least 10 leads, because only 10% will convert, you must have at least 10 leads.  This means that 10 leads * $30 per lead = $300, which is the absolute minimum budget we need to test an ad channel.

Be careful of small sample sizes

But, we’re not done. The problem is that we are looking at averages. It may be that amongst the 10 leads you first collect from an ad channel that none of them convert to sales. Afterall, when only an average of 1 out of 10 will convert to a sale, 1 is not too far off from 0.  Our sample size just may be too small.

img credit: Micah Sittig

This is like saying, if we flip a two-sided coin twice, we would expect heads to show up on average once. But, what if it shows up 0 times? Does that mean the coin is broken if heads doesn’t show up?  No, our sample size of flips is just too small.

The same thing applies to our case with our lead generation.  If we have 10 leads and on average we get one sale, does this mean an ad channel is bad if we get 0 sales from exactly 10 leads? No, our sample size is not big enough. My personal rule of thumb is to double the number of minimum leads you need to have enough confidence that we’ve tested a channel enough.  Again, this is just my rule of thumb.  So, in this case, I would recommend getting at least 20 leads to see if we are able to get at least one sale from this channel. This means that in this example, we need a test ad budget of 20 leads * $30 per lead = $600 minimum test budget.

Again, this is just for this particular example.  How big your sample size of leads needs to be is at your discretion. But, this is the kind of quick math you should be doing to figure out the right test budget size.
